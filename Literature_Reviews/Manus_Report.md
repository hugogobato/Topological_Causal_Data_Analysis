# A Literature Review on Causal Inference and Topological Data Analysis: Towards Topological Causal Data Analysis

## Abstract

This literature review explores the intersection of Causal Inference (CI) and Topological Data Analysis (TDA), two fields independently crucial for understanding complex data. CI provides frameworks for establishing cause-and-effect relationships, while TDA offers robust methods for extracting shape-based features from data. Traditional CI often focuses on mean-based effects, potentially overlooking significant structural changes in data distributions. TDA, through tools like Persistent Homology and the Mapper algorithm, can reveal these subtle topological alterations. Recent research has begun to bridge these fields, with advancements in topological causal discovery, such as Mahadevan's Causal Homotopy, and the estimation of topological causal effects, as demonstrated by Saki & Faghihi and Kim & Lee. These efforts highlight the potential to capture causal impacts that manifest as changes in the shape or connectivity of data manifolds. However, significant open problems remain, including the identifiability of marginal topological effects, scalability, robustness, and interpretability. This review culminates in proposing **Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA)** as a novel research domain, motivated by the need for a more comprehensive causal understanding that embraces the geometric and topological richness of data. TCDA aims to provide tools and theories to uncover and quantify causal relationships that manifest as fundamental alterations in the topological structure of complex systems.


## 1. Introduction

The fields of Causal Inference (CI) and Topological Data Analysis (TDA) have independently advanced our understanding of complex data. Causal Inference provides rigorous frameworks for establishing cause-and-effect relationships from observational and experimental data, moving beyond mere correlations [1]. Pioneering work by Judea Pearl and Donald Rubin laid the groundwork for modern CI, with Pearl's Structural Causal Models (SCMs) and Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) offering a graphical language for causality, and Rubin's Potential Outcomes framework providing a statistical lens [2, 3]. Recent years have seen a surge in integrating CI with machine learning, leading to advancements in causal discovery and effect estimation in high-dimensional settings [4, 5].

Simultaneously, Topological Data Analysis has emerged as a powerful suite of techniques for extracting robust, shape-based features from complex datasets [6]. TDA, primarily through tools like Persistent Homology (PH) and the Mapper algorithm, allows researchers to identify and quantify topological invariants such as connected components, loops, and voids, providing insights into the underlying geometric structure of data [7, 8]. The application of TDA has expanded rapidly, influencing areas from materials science to neuroscience and increasingly intersecting with deep learning [9, 10].

While both CI and TDA offer unique perspectives on data, their intersection remains a nascent but promising area of research. Traditional causal inference often focuses on changes in mean outcomes or distributional shifts, potentially overlooking more subtle, yet significant, alterations in the *shape* or *connectivity* of data manifolds induced by causal interventions. This literature review aims to explore the current state of this intersection, highlighting existing efforts in topological causal discovery and the estimation of topological causal effects. We will identify open problems and questions that arise from this interdisciplinary convergence and, building upon these insights, propose a novel research domain: **Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA)**. TCDA seeks to integrate the strengths of both fields to uncover and quantify causal relationships that manifest as changes in the topological structure of data, offering a richer and more comprehensive understanding of complex systems.


## 2. Foundations of Causal Inference

### 2.1 Key Frameworks and Concepts

Causal Inference (CI) is a scientific discipline dedicated to identifying and quantifying cause-and-effect relationships, distinguishing them from mere correlations. Two dominant frameworks underpin modern CI: Judea Pearl's Structural Causal Models (SCMs) and Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), and Donald Rubin's Potential Outcomes framework [2, 3].

**Pearl's Framework** centers on the idea that causal relationships can be represented by a system of structural equations and visualized as DAGs. In these graphs, nodes represent variables, and directed edges indicate direct causal influence. Key concepts within this framework include:

*   **Causal Discovery**: The process of inferring the underlying causal graph from observational or experimental data. This often involves identifying conditional independencies among variables [11].
*   **d-separation**: A graphical criterion used to determine conditional independencies between variables in a DAG, which is crucial for identifying causal effects [2].
*   **do-calculus**: A set of rules that allows for the computation of causal effects from observational data, even when direct experimentation is not possible, by formally distinguishing between observing an event and actively intervening to cause it [2].

**Rubin's Potential Outcomes Framework**, also known as the Neyman-Rubin model, defines causal effects in terms of counterfactuals. For each unit, there are potential outcomes under different treatment conditions, only one of which is observed. The causal effect is then defined as the difference between these potential outcomes. Key concepts include:

*   **Average Treatment Effect (ATE)**: The average difference between potential outcomes if all units received treatment versus if all units received control.
*   **Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE)**: The average treatment effect within specific subgroups defined by covariates.
*   **Ignorability**: A crucial assumption stating that, conditional on observed covariates, treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes, allowing for unbiased estimation of causal effects [3].

### 2.2 Recent Advances and Challenges

The field of Causal Inference has witnessed significant advancements, particularly with its integration into machine learning (ML). This has led to the emergence of **Causal Machine Learning**, which leverages ML algorithms for more robust and flexible causal effect estimation and causal discovery in complex, high-dimensional datasets [4, 5]. Techniques such as Double Machine Learning (DML) have been developed to provide valid inference for causal parameters even when ML models are used for nuisance parameter estimation [4].

Despite these advances, several challenges persist. Causal discovery, especially in the absence of strong assumptions or interventional data, remains a hard problem. The identifiability of causal effects often relies on untestable assumptions like ignorability and faithfulness. Furthermore, traditional CI methods primarily focus on changes in the *mean* or *variance* of outcomes, potentially overlooking more intricate, structural changes in data distributions that might be causally induced [12]. The scalability of causal discovery algorithms to very large numbers of variables and the handling of unobserved confounders continue to be active areas of research [11].

## 3. Foundations of Topological Data Analysis

### 3.1 Key Tools and Concepts

Topological Data Analysis (TDA) is a rapidly evolving field that applies concepts from algebraic topology to extract robust, qualitative features from complex datasets [6]. The core idea behind TDA is to study the "shape" of data, which can reveal underlying structures and patterns that might be missed by traditional statistical methods focusing on metrics like mean or variance [13]. The primary tools in TDA include Persistent Homology and the Mapper algorithm.

**Persistent Homology (PH)** is a fundamental tool in TDA that quantifies the topological features (e.g., connected components, loops, voids) of a dataset across multiple spatial scales [7]. It works by constructing a sequence of simplicial complexes (a filtration) over the data, typically by varying a distance parameter. As this parameter changes, topological features appear and disappear. PH tracks the "birth" and "death" of these features, recording them in a **Persistence Diagram (PD)**. A PD is a multiset of points in a 2D plane, where each point (birth, death) corresponds to a topological feature, with its longevity (persistence) indicating its robustness [7, 14].

The **Mapper Algorithm** is another widely used TDA technique that provides a simplified, graph-based representation of high-dimensional data [8]. It involves three main steps: (1) defining a filter function to project data onto a lower-dimensional space, (2) covering the range of the filter function with overlapping intervals, and (3) clustering data points within each interval and connecting clusters if they share common data points. The resulting Mapper graph provides a concise and interpretable summary of the data's global structure and connectivity [8, 15].

### 3.2 Recent Advances and Challenges

TDA has seen significant advancements and a broadening of its application domains. Recent trends include the development of **Topological Deep Learning**, where topological concepts are integrated into neural network architectures to enhance their ability to learn and generalize from structured data, particularly in fields like medical imaging and natural language processing [9, 10]. Statistical methods for analyzing persistence diagrams are also gaining traction, allowing for more rigorous hypothesis testing and comparison of topological features across different datasets [16].

Despite its growing popularity, TDA faces several challenges. One major issue is the **parameter sensitivity** of some TDA algorithms, particularly the Mapper algorithm, where the choice of filter function, number of intervals, and clustering method can significantly influence the resulting topological representation [17]. The **computational cost** of persistent homology, especially for large and high-dimensional datasets, can also be prohibitive, although ongoing research is focused on developing more efficient algorithms [18]. Furthermore, the **interpretability** of persistence diagrams and other topological summaries can be challenging for non-experts, requiring specialized knowledge to translate topological features back into meaningful insights about the data [19].

## 4. The Intersection of Causal Inference and Topological Data Analysis

### 4.1 Topological Approaches to Causal Discovery

The integration of TDA into causal discovery aims to leverage topological insights to better infer causal structures from data. One notable approach is **Mahadevan's "Causal Homotopy"** [20]. This framework utilizes Alexandroff topologies to represent causal Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), exploiting the close connections between partially ordered sets (posets) and finite Alexandroff topologies. By constructing a topological representation of posets from data, and then using this representation to build conventional DAG causal models, Mahadevan demonstrates that the homotopy theory of Alexandroff spaces can significantly reduce the number of possible DAG structures, thereby shrinking the search space for causal discovery algorithms by several orders of magnitude [20]. This work suggests that topological separability constraints on datasets can be instrumental in inferring causal structure from observations and interventions.

### 4.2 Topological Causal Effects

Beyond causal discovery, TDA is also being explored to define and estimate novel types of causal effects that go beyond traditional mean-based measures. **Saki and Faghihi (2026)** introduce the concept of **"Topological Causal Effects" (TCE)** in their paper "Beyond Means: Topological Causal Effects under Persistent-Homology Ignorability" [12]. They argue that traditional causal estimands like Average Treatment Effect (ATE) and Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) primarily target changes in expected outcomes and can miss treatment-induced changes in the *shape* or *geometry* of outcome distributions. For instance, a treatment might transform a unimodal outcome distribution into a bimodal one without altering its mean, a change that mean-based metrics would fail to capture [12].

To address this, Saki and Faghihi develop a topological causal framework based on persistent homology. They formalize a **"Persistent-Homology Ignorability"** condition and define topological analogues of CATE and ATE. They prove that these estimands are identifiable, up to an explicit error bound, under approximate topological ignorability. Their synthetic experiments demonstrate that while mean-based causal estimands remain near zero in scenarios with mean-preserving topology changes, the proposed topological effect sharply increases and is recoverable after adjustment for confounding [12].

Another contribution in this area is by **Kwangho Kim and Hajin Lee (2026)**, who also propose a framework for **Topological Causal Effects** to estimate causal effects for non-Euclidean outcomes, such as molecular graphs [21]. Their method defines treatment effects through differences in the topological structure of potential outcomes, summarized by power-weighted silhouette functions of persistence diagrams. They develop a doubly robust estimator in a fully nonparametric model and establish functional weak convergence, enabling a formal test of the null hypothesis of no topological effect [21].

### 4.3 Other Intersections and Perspectives

**Ibeling (2021)**, in "A Topological Perspective on Causal Inference," introduces a series of topologies defined on general spaces of Structural Causal Models (SCMs) [22]. This work presents a topological learning-theoretic perspective on causal inference and proves a **topological causal hierarchy theorem**. This theorem suggests that substantive assumption-free causal inference is possible only in a meager set of SCMs, implying that inductive assumptions sufficient for valid causal inferences are statistically unverifiable in principle [22]. This highlights the fundamental role of assumptions in causal inference from a topological viewpoint.

Furthermore, TDA has been combined with techniques like Delay Coordinate Embedding for causal inference in empirical dynamical systems, aiming to understand causal relationships in time-series data by analyzing the topological properties of reconstructed phase spaces [23]. These diverse approaches underscore the growing recognition of topology's potential to enrich causal analysis.

## 5. Open Problems and Future Directions

The nascent field at the intersection of Causal Inference and Topological Data Analysis presents numerous open problems and exciting avenues for future research. Addressing these challenges will be crucial for the development of a robust Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA) framework.

One significant challenge lies in the **identifiability of marginal persistence-diagram effects** from conditional topological ignorability. As noted by Saki and Faghihi [12], persistent homology does not generally commute with mixtures over covariates, making it difficult to directly infer marginal topological effects from conditional ones. Future work needs to explore methods for robustly identifying and estimating these marginal effects, potentially through novel theoretical developments or advanced statistical techniques that account for the non-commutative nature of PH with mixtures.

**Scalability** remains a practical concern. The computational cost of persistent homology, especially when applied to large-scale datasets or complex causal graphs, can be prohibitive [18]. Developing more efficient algorithms for computing PH on massive datasets, or devising approximation techniques that retain crucial topological information, will be essential for the widespread adoption of TCDA. This also extends to causal discovery algorithms that incorporate topological features, where the search space for causal structures can still be vast.

**Robustness** to noise and parameter sensitivity is another critical area. TDA methods, particularly the Mapper algorithm, can be sensitive to the choice of parameters (e.g., filter functions, number of intervals, clustering methods) [17]. Ensuring that the extracted topological features are robust to variations in these parameters and to inherent noise in the data is vital for reliable causal inference. Research into robust parameter selection strategies and uncertainty quantification for topological features in causal contexts is needed.

The **interpretability** of topological features in a causal context poses a conceptual challenge. While persistent homology can identify voids, loops, and connected components, translating these abstract topological features back into meaningful physical or mechanistic causal insights is not always straightforward [19]. Developing methodologies that bridge the gap between abstract topological representations and concrete causal mechanisms will enhance the utility and understanding of TCDA.

Finally, a major open problem is the **integration** of existing topological causal discovery and effect estimation methods into a unified and coherent framework for "Topological Causal Data Analysis" (TCDA). This involves developing a comprehensive theoretical foundation that seamlessly combines the strengths of both CI and TDA, allowing for both the discovery of causal structures that manifest topologically and the estimation of causal effects on the shape of data. Such a framework would need to address how topological features can be incorporated into causal models, how interventions affect these features, and how to infer these relationships from data.

## 6. Motivation for Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA)

The preceding sections highlight a compelling motivation for establishing **Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA)** as a distinct and vital research domain. Traditional Causal Inference, while powerful in establishing cause-and-effect relationships, often operates under assumptions that may not fully capture the complexity of real-world phenomena. Specifically, CI methods predominantly focus on changes in central tendencies (e.g., means) or simple distributional shifts, potentially overlooking profound structural transformations in data induced by causal factors [12].

This limitation becomes particularly evident in complex systems where interventions might not alter the average outcome but fundamentally change the *connectivity*, *shape*, or *topology* of the data manifold. For instance, a drug might not change the average expression level of a gene but could reorganize the co-expression network of genes, leading to a different topological structure. Such changes, which are critical for understanding the underlying mechanisms, would be invisible to mean-based causal analyses.

TCDA offers a novel lens through which to investigate causality by integrating the shape-centric perspective of TDA with the rigorous inferential capabilities of CI. The core motivations for TCDA include:

*   **Capturing Shape-Based Causal Effects**: TCDA can detect and quantify causal impacts that manifest as alterations in the topological features of data, such as the emergence or disappearance of holes, loops, or connected components. This goes "beyond means" to provide a richer, more nuanced understanding of causal mechanisms [12].
*   **Robustness to Noise and Transformations**: Topological features, particularly those derived from persistent homology, are known for their robustness to noise and small perturbations in data [7]. This inherent stability can make TCDA a powerful tool for causal inference in noisy, high-dimensional datasets where traditional methods might struggle.
*   **Understanding Complex System Dynamics**: Many complex systems, from biological networks to social interactions, exhibit emergent properties that are best characterized by their global structure and connectivity rather than individual components. TCDA can provide insights into how causal interventions alter these systemic topological properties, offering a more holistic view of causality.
*   **Bridging the Gap between Correlation and Causation with Structure**: While TDA can reveal structural patterns, it does not inherently imply causation. By explicitly integrating CI frameworks, TCDA can move beyond merely describing topological correlations to rigorously inferring causal relationships that govern the shape and structure of data.
*   **Novel Applications in Diverse Fields**: The ability to identify topological causal effects opens doors for new applications in fields such as drug discovery (how a compound changes protein folding topology), climate science (how policies affect climate pattern connectivity), and social sciences (how interventions alter social network structures).

In essence, TCDA is motivated by the need for a more comprehensive causal understanding that embraces the geometric and topological richness of data. It aims to provide tools and theories to answer questions like: "Does intervention X causally alter the number of independent cycles in a network?" or "Does treatment Y induce a new manifold structure in the patient data?" By doing so, TCDA promises to unlock new insights into the fundamental mechanisms driving complex phenomena.

## 7. Conclusion

This literature review has explored the foundational principles of Causal Inference and Topological Data Analysis, and critically examined their emerging intersection. We have seen that while Causal Inference provides robust methodologies for establishing cause-and-effect relationships, it often focuses on mean-based or simple distributional changes. Conversely, Topological Data Analysis offers powerful tools to uncover the intrinsic shape and structure of data, revealing insights that traditional statistical methods might miss. The recent advancements in topological causal discovery and the estimation of topological causal effects, as exemplified by the work of Mahadevan, Saki & Faghihi, and Kim & Lee, underscore the immense potential of integrating these two fields.

However, this nascent interdisciplinary domain is not without its challenges. Open problems such as the identifiability of marginal topological effects, scalability issues, robustness to noise and parameter sensitivity, and the interpretability of topological features in a causal context require significant future research. Addressing these challenges will be paramount for the successful development and application of Topological Causal Data Analysis (TCDA).

Ultimately, the motivation for TCDA stems from the recognition that many causal phenomena in complex systems manifest not just as changes in average values, but as fundamental alterations in the underlying geometric and topological structure of data. By providing a framework to rigorously investigate these shape-based causal impacts, TCDA promises to offer a more comprehensive, nuanced, and insightful understanding of causality. This novel research domain holds the potential to unlock new discoveries across a wide array of scientific and engineering disciplines, pushing the boundaries of our ability to model, understand, and intervene in complex systems.
